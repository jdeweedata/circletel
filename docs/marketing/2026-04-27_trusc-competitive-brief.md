# Competitive Brief: TRUSC
**Prepared:** 2026-04-27  
**Author:** CircleTel Marketing (Claude-assisted)  
**Research basis:** Web research + 1,470-contract TRUSC billing dataset (internal)  
**Freshness:** April 2026

---

## 1. Executive Summary

TRUSC is a West Coast-focused fixed wireless ISP with 10+ years of presence in Saldanha Bay, Vredenburg, Langebaan, and surrounding towns. They have established coverage in CircleTel's primary target geography and hold a meaningful customer base that our internal dataset puts at 1,470+ active business contracts. However, TRUSC's reputation is deteriorating — a HelloPeter TrustIndex of **2.1/5** signals deep customer satisfaction problems, and their technology stack (shared, unlicensed spectrum) is structurally inferior to Tarana FWA.

**Biggest opportunity:** TRUSC's own unhappy customers are the market. The campaign doesn't need to create demand — it needs to reach existing TRUSC customers who are already frustrated, already price-aware, and actively looking for alternatives.

**Biggest threat:** TRUSC may respond to customer defection with aggressive retention pricing or lock-in incentives. Their switching cost (existing CPE, 24-month contracts) creates friction we must explicitly address.

## 2. Competitor Profile: TRUSC

### Company Overview
- **What they do:** Fixed wireless and FTTH/FTTB broadband ISP serving the West Coast of the Western Cape
- **Target audience:** Residential and SME customers in underserved West Coast towns; positioned as the "default" ISP in markets where Telkom fibre is unavailable or delayed
- **Coverage:** Springbok to Cape Town corridor along the N7/West Coast; concentrated presence in Saldanha Bay municipality, Hopefield, Velddrif, Paternoster, Langebaan
- **Company stage:** Private; no public funding information; operates as a regional ISP, likely 10–50 employees
- **Key recent development:** No major product launches or expansions detected (2025–2026); company appears to be in maintenance mode rather than growth mode

### Messaging Analysis

| Element | TRUSC's Approach |
|---|---|
| **Primary positioning** | "Premier Wireless Internet Service Provider" — coverage-first messaging |
| **Core value prop** | Availability: "we cover areas others don't" |
| **Key themes** | Local coverage, wireless alternative to fibre, community presence |
| **Tone / voice** | Functional, utility-focused; no strong brand personality |
| **Problem framing** | "No fibre in your area? We have you covered" |
| **Weakest message** | Performance and reliability — conspicuously absent from messaging |

**Observation:** TRUSC leads with coverage availability, not performance or value. This is a legacy positioning built when "any internet" was the customer need. In 2026, the customer need has shifted to *reliable, fast* internet — and TRUSC's messaging hasn't kept pace.

### Product and Technology Positioning

| Dimension | TRUSC |
|---|---|
| **Primary technology** | Fixed wireless (unlicensed spectrum + some FTTH/FTTB) |
| **Spectrum type** | Unlicensed (shared, susceptible to congestion) |
| **Speeds marketed** | Up to ~25Mbps (wireless); higher on fibre routes |
| **SLA** | Not prominently advertised |
| **Pricing (public)** | R199–R299/mo residential entry; business tiers estimated R500–R2,000+/mo based on internal contract data |
| **Contract terms** | Month-to-month and annual options observed |
| **Hardware** | CPE provided; no mention of customer-owned hardware option |

**Critical weakness:** Unlicensed shared spectrum means TRUSC customers experience peak-hour congestion — the same problem that plagued early LTE networks. As more customers join the network, existing customers suffer. This is a structural problem TRUSC cannot fix without rebuilding their network.

### Content and Market Presence

| Channel | TRUSC Activity |
|---|---|
| **Website** | trusc.co.za / trusc.net — functional, not conversion-optimised; no pricing published |
| **Facebook** | @TruscISP — active but reactive (mostly responding to complaints) |
| **LinkedIn** | Minimal presence; no thought leadership |
| **Blog / content** | No evidence of content marketing programme |
| **SEO** | Weak; no visible keyword strategy; brand searches only |
| **Reviews (HelloPeter)** | TrustIndex **2.1/5** — significant complaint volume around billing, support response times, and service outages |
| **PR / news** | No press releases or media coverage found (2024–2026) |

### Strengths

1. **Established coverage infrastructure:** Physical towers and base stations in place across the West Coast — this took years and capital to build; cannot be replicated overnight
2. **First-mover incumbency:** Many West Coast businesses have been TRUSC customers for 5–10 years; switching inertia is real
3. **Local presence / relationships:** Known in the community; some customers have personal relationships with TRUSC staff
4. **Geographic monopoly in some towns:** In villages like Paternoster and Jacobsbaai, TRUSC may be the only available option — giving them no competitive pressure

### Weaknesses

1. **Customer satisfaction crisis:** HelloPeter 2.1/5 is a red flag. Billing complaints, support delays, and outage frustration are recurring themes. This creates ready-made defection intent.
2. **Shared unlicensed spectrum:** Structural performance ceiling — the more customers they add, the worse existing customers' experience becomes
3. **No SLA marketed:** Business customers paying R800–R2,000/mo expect guaranteed uptime; TRUSC does not appear to offer this
4. **Pricing opacity:** No public pricing creates friction for prospects and prevents comparison — but also signals TRUSC's pricing is not competitive
5. **Zero content / SEO strategy:** TRUSC has no digital presence beyond their website; they rely entirely on word-of-mouth and existing customer retention
6. **No product innovation signals:** No new products, partnerships, or technology investments visible in 2024–2026

## 6. Threats

| Threat | Assessment |
|---|---|
| **TRUSC price match / retention offers** | If TRUSC becomes aware of the campaign, they may offer discounts to at-risk customers. Mitigation: lead with non-price advantages (spectrum quality, SLA, local support) so the value proposition doesn't collapse if they price-match |
| **TRUSC's physical infrastructure advantage** | Their towers are already built. In towns where Tarana base stations don't yet exist, TRUSC remains the only option — we cannot pitch Tarana to customers we can't serve |
| **Switching friction (existing CPE, contracts)** | Customers with 12–24 month TRUSC contracts face cancellation penalties. Must explicitly address: offer to cover penalty costs up to a threshold, or time outreach to contract expiry |
| **Brand awareness gap** | CircleTel is less well-known in some West Coast towns than TRUSC. First-touch outreach may need to build trust before pitching |
| **MTN deal catalogue expiry** | Current MTN Arlan catalogue expired 6 April 2026. Without current deal IDs, List B (1,090 records) campaign cannot launch. This is an immediate operational blocker. |

## 7. Recommended Actions

### Quick Wins (this week)

1. **Register `/switch-from-trusc` as a campaign URL and build the landing page.**  
   This is the single highest-leverage move: zero ad spend, captures intent, gives sales a credible link to share. Include: savings calculator, comparison table, "Why CircleTel?" section, lead form.

2. **Set up HelloPeter monitoring for TRUSC complaints.**  
   Use a free HelloPeter alert or manual check 2× per week. Respond to negative TRUSC reviews as CircleTel — introduce the brand, offer to help. This reaches the most motivated switchers at zero cost.

3. **Draft WhatsApp outreach sequence for top 50 List A accounts.**  
   The top 50 Tarana-coverable accounts by monthly fee represent ~R35,000+/month in addressable revenue. Reach them personally before spending on paid ads.

### Strategic Moves (this month)

4. **Publish 2 SEO-targeted blog posts:**  
   - "Fixed wireless vs dedicated spectrum: what West Coast businesses need to know"  
   - "Switching ISP in South Africa: how to cancel TRUSC and what to expect"  
   These will compound in search over 6–12 months and create perpetual inbound intent capture.

5. **Run a per-address Tarana coverage check (not town centroid).**  
   Current List A is based on town-centroid distance to base stations. A per-address check using `find_nearest_tarana_base_station` will sharpen the list — removing false positives and potentially recovering addresses flagged as "out of range" that are actually within 10km of a BN.

### Longer-Term

6. **Build a TRUSC contract expiry tracker.**  
   If contract dates can be inferred from contract start dates in the dataset, pre-schedule outreach to arrive 60 days before contract renewal — the moment switching cost is lowest and switching intent is highest.
