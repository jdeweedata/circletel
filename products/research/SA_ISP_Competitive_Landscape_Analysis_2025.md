# South Africa ISP competitive landscape: a comprehensive analysis for CircleTel positioning

**South Africa's internet market is undergoing rapid consolidation and structural transformation, creating both threats and significant openings for mid-tier ISPs.** The country's **2.47 million FTTH/FTTB subscribers** (up from 1.49 million in 2023) generated R6.5 billion in revenue in 2024, while the broader telecoms sector reached R232 billion. The distinctive open-access fibre model — where Fibre Network Operators (FNOs) build infrastructure and ISPs resell connectivity — remains the defining feature of the market, though vertical integration pressure from the Vodacom-Maziv mega-deal is reshaping competitive dynamics. For a mid-tier ISP like CircleTel, the landscape presents a paradox: entry-level consumer fibre has commoditised (R249–R499/month), yet **the business segment remains opaque, high-margin, and underserved by nimble players**, while lower-income and semi-urban areas represent the last major growth frontier with 0.4% fibre penetration across 5 million addressable homes.

---

## 1. The SA ISP model: how FNOs and ISPs divide the market

South Africa operates a globally distinctive **open-access fibre model** that separates physical infrastructure from retail service delivery. FNOs build and maintain fibre networks (Layer 1–2), while ISPs purchase wholesale access and sell retail packages (Layer 3) with billing, support, and value-added services. This creates ISP-level competition even where only one FNO operates — though in practice, **~80% of neighbourhoods are served by a single FNO**, limiting true consumer choice to ISP selection rather than infrastructure.

The market's six major FNOs have collectively passed approximately **6.1 million homes**, with Vumatel dominating at 2.04 million homes passed (plus 586,000 via its newly acquired Herotel subsidiary). Openserve (Telkom) follows with 1.5 million homes passed but leads on **connectivity ratio at 52.4%** — meaning it converts more passed homes to paying subscribers than any competitor. MetroFibre (510,000), Frogfoot (406,000), and Octotel (372,000) round out the major players, each with between 140 and 50+ ISP partners on their networks.

The model's core advantage is competition on service quality rather than infrastructure duplication. Its weakness is FNO pricing power: wholesale rates are set by FNOs with limited regulatory oversight, squeezing ISP margins. The ISPA (Internet Service Providers' Association) has actively lobbied against exclusive estate contracts and FNO practices that obstruct ISP switching. Openserve and Link Africa score highest for open-access adherence (7.3/10 in ISPA surveys), while **Vumatel received the lowest rating among major FNOs** (4.7/10) despite being the largest.

## 2. Business ISP segment: enterprise connectivity remains high-margin and relationship-driven

The business ISP segment in South Africa is dominated by three mobile-network-backed giants — Vodacom Business, MTN Business, and Telkom/BCX — supported by a tier of specialist infrastructure and managed-services players. Enterprise pricing is almost universally opaque (quote-based), creating both a barrier and an opportunity for transparent challengers.

### The big three control the enterprise market

**Vodacom Business** operates as South Africa's largest enterprise ICT provider, backed by 50.7 million mobile subscribers and Vodafone's global technology stack. Its full-stack offering spans business fibre (10–200 Mbps on 11+ third-party FNOs), SD-WAN (Cisco Meraki partnership deployed across 47 African countries), MPLS, cloud marketplace services (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud direct connect), cybersecurity, IoT (nationwide NB-IoT), and managed IT. Consumer fibre starts at ~R399/month; business fibre is bespoke. Vodacom's R12 billion annual capex and R60 billion five-year investment plan underpin aggressive 5G expansion (~52% population coverage). Its attempted acquisition of Maziv/DFA was initially blocked by the Competition Tribunal in October 2024 but ultimately **approved by the Competition Appeal Court in August 2025**, with Vodacom taking a co-controlling stake effective December 2025 — a transformative move giving the mobile giant access to South Africa's largest fibre infrastructure. Annual price increases of 4.5–9% reflect Vodacom's premium positioning.

**MTN Business** leverages 39.2 million SA mobile subscribers and the fastest average mobile download speed in the country (**82.48 Mbps**). MTN's enterprise portfolio includes pan-African MPLS (16 markets), the country's first Cloudflare MSSP partnership (August 2025) for Zero Trust security, cloud and data centre services, and IoT (73,000+ deployed devices). MTN has aggressively cut fibre prices (50% reductions) and launched Shesha 5G plans from just **R330/month** — among the market's most competitive 5G offerings. Its business broadband fibre targets mall tenants on MTN's own GPON network, with broader fibre sold via Vumatel and Openserve. MTN's consumer brand Supersonic has poor customer satisfaction ratings, but the enterprise division benefits from network speed leadership and month-to-month contract flexibility.

**Telkom Business / BCX / Openserve** brings 140+ years of fixed-line incumbency and the advantage of owning Openserve, SA's second-largest FNO. BCX, Telkom's enterprise ICT subsidiary (6,000+ employees, R14.2 billion revenue in FY2024), provides system integration, managed IT, SAP services, cloud platforms, and cybersecurity through its own Security Operations Centre. Telkom offers the **most affordable business fibre entry point at R315/month** (20/10 Mbps Easy Connect), though a 12% average price increase from April 2025 was the steepest in the market. BCX International revenue exceeded R1 billion for the first time. The group is actively seeking an equity partner for BCX and exploring Openserve investment, signalling strategic restructuring. Group revenue reached R43.9 billion (+3.3%) with EBITDA of R11.8 billion (+25.1%) in FY2025.

### Specialist enterprise and infrastructure players

**NTT DATA (formerly Dimension Data)** rebranded in April 2024 and operates as South Africa's premier enterprise IT services provider — 8,000+ employees across 15 MEA countries, serving 65% of Fortune 500 companies. NTT's offering centres on managed services via its AI-driven SPEKTRA platform (reducing incidents by 87%), hybrid cloud, SD-WAN/SASE, SAP migration, and cybersecurity. Its 11+ data centre facilities across Africa include the Johannesburg 1 Tier 3 facility (12 MW). NTT is not a traditional ISP but a **managed services and systems integration powerhouse**, making it a competitor primarily in the large-enterprise, high-value segment.

**Seacom** owns Africa's most extensive submarine cable infrastructure — **35,000 km of backbone, 6 subsea cables, 38 PoPs across 17 markets** — and serves 4,000+ enterprise customers including Standard Bank, MTN, Vodacom, Google, and Netflix. Its enterprise portfolio includes dedicated internet access (10–100 Gbps), CloudWorx (private cloud connectivity to AWS/Azure), corporate Ethernet, and colocation across 8 facilities. Enterprise SLAs guarantee 99–99.9% uptime with symmetrical, 1:1 contention ratios. Seacom's consumer arm, WonderNet, provides FTTH. The Seabeyond 2030 strategy targets new subsea systems, data centres, and AI-ready services.

**Teraco** dominates as Africa's largest carrier-neutral data centre platform — **189 MW of critical IT load across 8 facilities** in Johannesburg, Cape Town, and Durban. Its JB4 facility (50 MW) is Africa's largest standalone data centre. Teraco hosts 650+ organisations including all major hyperscalers, operates NAPAfrica (Africa's largest Internet Exchange Point at 6 Tbps peak traffic with 656+ unique ASNs), and provides the Africa Cloud Exchange for virtual connections to AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud. Backed by Digital Realty (NYSE: DLR), Teraco's recent expansions include CT2 (+32 MW), JB4 (+30 MW), and a 120 MW solar plant. While not an ISP, Teraco is the **critical interconnection hub** through which most South African internet traffic flows.

### Mid-tier business ISPs carve specialist niches

**Vox Business** (Vivica Group, which also owns Frogfoot) offers the most transparent business fibre pricing in the market. Multi-tenant business fibre ranges from **R1,099/month (20 Mbps) to R4,599/month (1 Gbps)**, while dedicated single-tenant connections start at R3,199/month. Premium packages include **99.9% SLA, 4-hour response, 12-hour restore**, and 1:1 access contention. Vox's portfolio spans FTTB, Cloud PBX (Vobi App with 60 free minutes), SD-WAN, managed WiFi, and satellite internet — making it the most complete mid-tier business offering.

**RSAWEB Business** operates from Cape Town with dedicated FTTB products featuring dual SLAs (from both RSAWEB and the underlying FNO). Business fibre pricing is quote-based via account managers, with tiers ranging from FTTB Lite (best-effort FNO SLA) to FTTB Premium (full business-grade SLAs from both layers). RSAWEB's 4.7/5 Google rating (23,000+ reviews) — the highest of any SA ISP — and #1 Netflix Speed Index ranking reflect technical excellence that translates into SMB/enterprise credibility.

**Comsol Networks** occupies a unique position as SA's leading enterprise FWA provider, operating licensed mmWave (28 GHz) and 3.7 GHz spectrum across 200+ towns. Comsol's open-access Layer 2 model serves carriers, ISPs, and enterprises with symmetrical 10 Mbps to 10 Gbps connections. Its 2024 launch of business-class 5G FWA (with 24-hour battery autonomy on base stations) and Ericsson Private 5G partnership for mining represents the cutting edge of enterprise wireless in South Africa. Comsol operates Africa's largest open-access LPWAN for IoT.

**Rain Business** (rainOne Work, launched June 2024) targets SMBs with unlimited 5G WiFi packages from **R795/month (30 Mbps)** including 5 free 4G mobile SIMs. While affordable, Rain offers no SLAs, no PBX/VoIP, and WhatsApp-only support — limiting its appeal to cost-conscious micro-businesses rather than enterprises requiring guaranteed uptime.

## 3. Consumer ISP segment: commoditisation at speed, differentiation on service

The consumer market has reached a mature phase where pricing has largely converged, FNO availability determines coverage, and **customer service quality has become the primary differentiator**. The MyBroadband ISP satisfaction survey — South Africa's most cited consumer benchmark — shows Afrihost leading at 76.29/100, with Cool Ideas dominating at 86% on WhichVoIP. At the bottom, Telkom (56%) and MTN/Supersonic (overwhelmingly negative Trustpilot reviews) demonstrate that network size does not guarantee customer loyalty.

### Afrihost: the market's satisfaction champion

Afrihost, 7-time MyBroadband ISP of the Year, operates across **24+ FNO networks** — the widest coverage of any consumer ISP. Its "Pure Fibre" branding emphasises uncapped, unshaped, unthrottled connectivity. Entry pricing starts at **R329/month (Openserve 10/5 Mbps)** and scales to R2,347/month (Vumatel 1 Gbps). All packages are month-to-month with free installation on most FNOs (saving up to R5,000). Afrihost also operates Air Mobile (MVNO on MTN) and hosting services. The ISP's digital-first approach and 250,000+ customer base make it the benchmark against which all consumer ISPs are measured. Notably, Afrihost acquired a majority stake in Cool Ideas, creating a two-brand strategy spanning value (Afrihost) and premium/enthusiast (Cool Ideas) segments.

### Cool Ideas: the enthusiast's ISP

Winner of the Ookla Speedtest Award for Best Fixed ISP in SA (2024, average **271 Mbps down / 208 Mbps up**) and MyBroadband Best Fibre ISP for three consecutive years, Cool Ideas operates across 6 FNO networks. Pricing runs from **R469/month (Vuma 30/30 Mbps)** to R2,239/month (Vuma 1 Gbps). Its 4.9/5 Hellopeter rating (1,900+ reviews) is the highest of any ISP. Cool Ideas also launched a 5G fixed wireless alternative from R199/month and peers at 11 IXPs (domestic and international) for optimal routing.

### Rain: the wireless disruptor

Rain operates Africa's first standalone 5G network and is the only major player offering **exclusively wireless home broadband** — no fibre at all. RainOne Home packages start at **R649/month (30 Mbps)** and include a free 5G router (the101 series, Wi-Fi 6/7) plus 2 free 4G mobile SIMs. The unlimited speed tier costs R1,049/month. Rain's 3,000+ own towers plus roaming on Vodacom and MTN provide 5G coverage across Gauteng and Cape Town, with 4G nationally. The brand has leaned into lifestyle positioning — designer router skins, a portable 5G device called "loop" (R5,999 + R475–R695/month), and an NVIDIA GeForce NOW gaming partnership. **Rain's key limitation is consistency**: customer reviews cite network dropouts and WhatsApp-only support as persistent issues.

### Webafrica and Mweb: value-oriented scale players

**Webafrica** (founded 1997, ~200,000 customers) partners with 20+ FNOs and offers aggressive R19 first-month promotions. Entry-level packages start at **R249/month (Vuma Reach 10/10 Mbps)**, with 1 Gbps options from R1,099 on Comtel. Its fully insured free router (covering lightning, breakage, and theft) and R999 credit guarantee if not installed within 14 days are unique service commitments. Webafrica Voice (VoIP) and a hardware shop extend the ecosystem.

**Mweb**, one of SA's oldest ISPs (founded 1997), is now a subsidiary of Webafrica and claims the **largest fibre subscriber base** among ISPs per Africa Analysis. Mweb operates across the widest range of FNOs and offers both postpaid and prepaid fibre from ~R299/month. With 35,000+ business customers and the Ask Africa Orange Index Winner for Best Customer Service ISP (three times), Mweb targets the mass-market family segment.

### Vodacom, MTN, and Telkom as consumer ISPs

**Vodacom's** consumer fibre starts at ~R499/month (20 Mbps) across its own self-build network plus 7+ third-party FNOs. Its differentiator is ecosystem bundling — gaming consoles (PS5, Nintendo Switch 2), tablets, mesh extenders, and a router with 4-hour loadshedding battery. Vodacom offers both month-to-month and 24-month contracts with promotional first-month discounts.

**MTN/Supersonic** is the most aggressive on pricing, with entry fibre from **~R289/month** (promotional) and its **AirFibre FWA product covering 3.4 million+ households** — by far the largest fixed wireless footprint in the country. AirFibre uses a patented A4-sized antenna and starts at R599/month, making it particularly strong in rural and township areas where fibre hasn't reached. However, Supersonic's customer service is among the worst-rated in the market.

**Telkom Internet** benefits from Openserve ownership but carries the legacy of a former state monopoly. Its uncapped Core Fibre starts at **R499/month (25/10 Mbps)**, while the budget Easy Connect tier offers 20/10 Mbps at R335/month (with FUP throttling). A prepaid Compact Fibre option (R199 for 14 days, 200 GB FUP) targets no-contract customers. Telkom's **12% average price increase in April 2025** was the steepest in the market, eroding its value positioning.

### Herotel: connecting rural South Africa

Herotel, SA's **largest fixed wireless internet provider** with 2,000+ towers and 586,000 homes passed across 500+ towns in all 9 provinces, was fully acquired by Vumatel (approved December 2025). As the only major closed-access network (both FNO and sole ISP on its infrastructure), Herotel's acquisition comes with conditions requiring a transition toward open access. Consumer fibre starts at R399/month (10 Mbps) with prepaid options from R99. The business division offers managed fibre, Cloud Call VoIP, heroFlash (Layer 2 bandwidth to Teraco), and dark fibre.

### WIRUlink/RUSH Network and Trusc: filling the wireless gap

**RUSH Network** (WIRUlink's open-access FWA infrastructure) covers **12 million people, 4 million homes, and 1.2 million businesses** primarily in Gauteng, with expansion into Bloemfontein and Northern Cape. ISP packages on RUSH start from R199/month (10 Mbps uncapped) with sub-15ms latency. Equipment installation costs R7,500+, positioning it as a fibre alternative/backup for underserved suburban areas. **Trusc Wireless** serves the West Coast of the Western Cape with fixed wireless, fibre, and PABX services — a hyper-local WISP model serving communities from Bitterfontein to Cape Town.

## 4. Key market trends reshaping SA connectivity (2024–2025)

### Fibre growth is shifting from rollout to densification

The upper-income fibre market is approaching saturation: **3.1 million of 3.2 million addressable affluent homes have been passed** (48.7% connected). Growth is now pivoting to middle-income areas (2.2 million of 4.3 million homes passed, 16.5% connected) and critically, to lower-income communities where only **100,000 of 5 million addressable homes** have fibre (0.4% connected). This explains the emergence of Fibertime (R35/week uncapped 100 Mbps, 250,000 homes passed by December 2025), Vuma Key (R99/month in Alexandra), and Vuma Reach — all targeting lower LSM households with simplified, prepaid-friendly products.

### Consolidation is accelerating at every level

The market saw transformative M&A activity in 2024–2025. The **Vodacom-Maziv deal** (approved August 2025, effective December 2025) gives South Africa's largest mobile operator co-control of its largest fibre infrastructure group, with conditions including R12 billion in fibre investment and 1 million new homes passed in lower-income areas over 5 years. **Vumatel's 100% acquisition of Herotel** (December 2025) creates a combined 2.6 million homes-passed juggernaut. The Actis exit from Octotel (March 2024) brought it under the AIIM umbrella alongside MetroFibre — effectively consolidating two major FNOs under common ownership. Afrihost's acquisition of Cool Ideas further consolidated the ISP retail layer.

### Prices are rising, not falling

After years of decline, **fibre prices began climbing in 2025** — driven by inflation, energy costs, a weak rand, and municipal tariff increases. Telkom's 12% average increase was the steepest, but Vumatel, Zoom Fibre (127% increase on entry-level packages), Herotel, and Vodacom (4.5–9%) all raised prices. Mobile data costs SA USD 2.67/GB (ranked 136th globally for affordability). The era of easy price-driven competition may be ending, forcing ISPs to differentiate on service and bundling rather than pure cost.

### 5G FWA is a genuine fibre competitor

With **46.64% population coverage** as of the ICASA 2025 report, 5G is becoming a credible fixed-broadband alternative. Rain pioneered the model; MTN's AirFibre now covers 3.4 million households; and Comsol's licensed-band 5G FWA offers enterprise-grade wireless with 24-hour battery autonomy. For areas without fibre infrastructure — still the majority of South Africa — FWA is not merely a stopgap but often the primary connectivity option. The Competition Tribunal's initial concerns about the Vodacom-Maziv deal centred partly on reduced FWA-versus-FTTH competition.

### Regulatory shifts favour infrastructure expansion but create uncertainty

ICASA's 2025 State of ICT Report confirmed telecoms revenue growth of 11.7% to R232 billion, driven by data services (56% of MNO revenue). Key regulatory developments include the effective deployment of the March 2022 spectrum (R14.4 billion auction), new call termination rate regulations from July 2025, and government plans for 2G/3G sunset by December 2027. The SA Connect Phase 2 programme targets 5.5 million connected households by end-2026, and the National Policy on Data and Cloud aims for 100% broadband coverage by 2030. ISPA continues to advocate against exclusive estate contracts, which remain a barrier to open competition.

## 5. Competitive implications for CircleTel's market positioning

### Where the gaps exist for a mid-tier ISP

The South African ISP market is simultaneously mature and underserved — mature in affluent urban areas where Afrihost, Cool Ideas, and RSAWEB compete fiercely on service quality, yet deeply underserved in three critical dimensions:

- **Business segment transparency**: Enterprise connectivity from Vodacom, MTN, and Seacom is universally quote-based and opaque. Vox is the only provider publishing transparent business fibre pricing. An ISP that offers **clear, public business pricing with guaranteed SLAs** — positioned between Telkom's R315/month entry and Vox's R1,099+ business tier — could capture SMBs frustrated by the RFQ process.

- **The middle-income residential frontier**: With only 16.5% of middle-income homes connected (2.2 million passed of 4.3 million addressable), this segment represents the largest absolute growth opportunity. These households can afford R400–R800/month but are underserved by FNOs still prioritising affluent suburbs.

- **Bundled value-added services**: Most consumer ISPs sell pure connectivity. Vox's VoIP bundling (60 free minutes) is a rare exception. An ISP that combines fibre with **VoIP, basic cybersecurity, smart home management, or estate-level services** can justify premium pricing while reducing churn.

### Strategic positioning options

A mid-tier ISP's optimal strategy depends on its existing infrastructure and capital position. Three viable models emerge from this analysis:

The **service-excellence model** (emulating Cool Ideas/RSAWEB) targets quality-conscious consumers and SMBs across multiple FNO networks. This requires minimal capital but demands exceptional support infrastructure — the 4.7–4.9/5 ratings that RSAWEB and Cool Ideas achieve are the primary competitive moat. Success here means operating across 6+ FNO networks with sub-4-hour fault coordination, transparent pricing, and human (not chatbot) support.

The **regional infrastructure model** (emulating Herotel/Evotel) involves deploying proprietary FWA or fibre infrastructure in underserved towns and secondary cities. Evotel's success in 21 secondary areas (Krugersdorp, Newcastle, Ladysmith, Welkom) and Fibertime's explosive growth in low-income areas (250,000 homes passed in three years from a 2022 start) demonstrate that first-mover advantage in uncovered areas is substantial. The capital requirement is higher, but the resulting FNO+ISP vertical integration delivers superior margins and customer lock-in.

The **business-focused model** (emulating Comsol/Vox) targets SMBs and enterprises with managed services, transparent SLAs, and differentiated technology. The enterprise segment offers significantly higher ARPU (R1,099–R11,499/month for business fibre versus R249–R997 for consumer) and stickier relationships. Pairing business fibre with Cloud PBX, managed security, and SD-WAN creates a recurring-revenue bundle that is difficult for pure-play consumer ISPs to replicate.

### Critical success factors in the current market

**Customer service is the moat.** In a market where pricing has converged and FNO availability is fixed, the ISPs with the highest satisfaction scores (Afrihost, Cool Ideas, RSAWEB) consistently gain share. MyBroadband and Hellopeter ratings function as de facto consumer guides. Any ISP scoring below 70% on MyBroadband or 4.0 on Hellopeter faces an uphill battle.

**Multi-FNO partnerships are non-negotiable** for consumer reach. Afrihost operates on 24+ networks; Webafrica on 20+. An ISP limited to one or two FNOs is geographically constrained and vulnerable to FNO pricing changes.

**Load shedding resilience matters operationally.** The industry spent R3.5 billion on backup power in 2023 alone. Comsol's 24-hour battery autonomy and Vodacom's 4-hour backup routers have become expected, not differentiating.

**The Vodacom-Maziv integration creates both threat and opportunity.** As Vodacom gains co-control of Vumatel's infrastructure, independent ISPs on Vumatel's network face potential competitive disadvantage. Diversifying FNO partnerships — particularly toward Openserve, Frogfoot, MetroFibre, and DFA — hedges this risk.

## Conclusion

South Africa's ISP market in 2025 is defined by three forces: infrastructure consolidation concentrating power among fewer FNOs, a pricing floor that has likely bottomed out after years of decline, and a vast untapped addressable market in middle- and lower-income areas. For CircleTel, the clearest strategic opportunities lie not in competing head-to-head with Afrihost on consumer fibre pricing but in exploiting the **business segment's opacity** (where transparent SLAs and pricing can win SMB loyalty), the **geographic whitespace** in secondary cities and peri-urban areas, and the **service-quality gap** that leaves Telkom, MTN/Supersonic, and even Vodacom vulnerable to smaller, more responsive competitors. The ISPs that will thrive in the next phase are those that treat connectivity as a platform for value-added services — not merely a pipe — while maintaining the operational discipline to deliver consistent, human-centred support at scale.
